In an era where AI-powered meeting tools routinely shuttle sensitive conversations through third-party cloud servers, **Meetily** takes a radically different approach. Built by Zackriya Solutions, this MIT-licensed meeting assistant captures, transcribes, and summarizes your meetings entirely on your own machine — no cloud, no telemetry, no data ever leaving your computer. It's a direct answer to the growing privacy anxiety around tools like Otter.ai and Fireflies.ai, designed for professionals and enterprises who treat confidentiality as table stakes, not a paid add-on.

Under the hood, Meetily is built in **Rust** for performance and reliability, with a clean frontend-backend architecture. It leverages Parakeet and Whisper (via whisper.cpp) for lightning-fast transcription — claiming up to **4× faster** than standard Whisper implementations — alongside speaker diarization powered by Sortformer to distinguish who said what. For AI summarization, it integrates seamlessly with **Ollama** for fully local LLM inference, while also supporting cloud providers like Claude, Groq, and OpenRouter if you prefer. The project has earned **16,926 ⭐** with 1,799 forks and 556 commits across 74 branches — a testament to active development and strong community traction.

Standout features include real-time live transcription during meetings, configurable AI summary generation, and multi-platform support across **macOS, Windows, and Linux**. It works with any meeting platform that captures system audio — Zoom, Teams, Google Meet, you name it — because it operates at the OS level rather than integrating per-app. The project also ships a "llama-helper" module for optimized local LLM token decoding, and an optional Meetily PRO tier for teams needing enhanced accuracy, custom summary workflows, and advanced exports.

Meetily's rise reflects a broader shift in developer sentiment: as AI capabilities grow more powerful, the demand for **sovereign, self-hosted alternatives** grows right alongside them. With nearly 17,000 stars and counting, it's clear that the community is hungry for tools that don't force a trade-off between intelligence and privacy.
